Best States for Solar Panels in 2026, Ranked by Savings

With the federal solar tax credit gone, state-level incentives, electricity rates, and sun hours now determine where solar panels deliver the biggest financial returns. Here are the 10 best states for going solar in 2026.

By GreenSage February 9, 2026 10 min read

Solar panel installations surged throughout the early 2020s, driven largely by the federal 30% Investment Tax Credit (ITC). But when the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) accelerated the expiration of the ITC at the end of 2025, homeowners lost what had been the single most valuable solar incentive in the country. A $25,000 system that once qualified for a $7,500 federal write-off now carries its full sticker price at the federal level.

That shift makes where you live more important than ever. The difference between installing solar in the best state versus the worst can mean $30,000 or more in lifetime savings. In 2026, the states that combine high electricity rates, generous state-level incentives, strong net metering laws, and abundant sunshine offer the greatest return on investment.

We analyzed all 50 states across four core ranking factors to identify the 10 best states for solar panels in 2026. Here is what we found.

How We Ranked the Best States for Solar

Every homeowner's situation is different, but four factors consistently determine whether solar is a smart financial decision:

1. Electricity Rates

This is the single most important variable. Solar panels save you money by replacing electricity you would otherwise buy from the grid. If your utility charges $0.30 per kilowatt-hour, every kWh your panels produce is worth $0.30. If your rate is only $0.10/kWh, that same kilowatt-hour saves you a third as much. States with high and rising electricity rates consistently deliver the best solar ROI, regardless of how much sun they get.

2. Peak Sun Hours

Peak sun hours measure how many hours per day your panels receive the equivalent of full-intensity sunlight (1,000 watts per square meter). Arizona averages 5.5 to 6.5 peak sun hours daily, while states like Michigan and Washington average closer to 3.5. More sun means more electricity production per installed kilowatt, which directly increases your savings and shortens payback periods.

3. State Incentives

With the federal ITC gone, state-level tax credits, rebates, and performance-based incentives now carry the heaviest weight. Some states offer upfront rebates that reduce installation costs by thousands of dollars. Others provide ongoing payments for the solar renewable energy certificates (SRECs) your system generates. The best states for solar in 2026 have strong, well-funded incentive programs that effectively replace much of the value the federal credit once provided.

4. Net Metering Policies

Net metering allows you to send excess solar electricity back to the grid and receive a credit on your utility bill. Under true 1:1 net metering, every kilowatt-hour you export is worth the same as every kilowatt-hour you import. Some states have moved to reduced net metering rates or time-of-use structures that pay less for exported energy. Strong net metering significantly improves solar economics, especially for systems that produce more than the homeowner consumes during daytime hours.

Key Takeaway

High electricity rates matter more than sunshine. A state with $0.28/kWh rates and moderate sun will almost always deliver better solar savings than a state with $0.10/kWh rates and excellent sun.

The 10 Best States for Solar Panels in 2026

1. California

Average electricity rate: $0.30+/kWh | Peak sun hours: 5.5/day | 25-year savings: $45,000 - $65,000

California remains the top state for solar in 2026, though its incentive landscape has shifted. Electricity rates are among the highest in the nation, regularly exceeding $0.30/kWh for residential customers on tiered rate plans. For households on Tier 2 or Tier 3 pricing, effective rates can reach $0.40 to $0.50 per kilowatt-hour, making solar panels enormously valuable.

The state's Self-Generation Incentive Program (SGIP) provides rebates for battery storage paired with solar, which is particularly relevant under the NEM 3.0 framework that took effect for new solar installations in April 2023. NEM 3.0 reduced the value of exported solar electricity significantly compared to the old NEM 2.0 program, paying roughly $0.05 to $0.08/kWh for exports instead of full retail rate. However, the combination of extremely high retail rates, excellent sun exposure, and battery storage incentives means California homeowners who pair solar with a battery system still see some of the best returns in the country.

2. Massachusetts

Average electricity rate: $0.28/kWh | Peak sun hours: 4.0/day | 25-year savings: $40,000 - $55,000

Massachusetts proves that you do not need desert sunshine to make solar worthwhile. With average electricity rates near $0.28/kWh and winter rates that spike above $0.30/kWh, every kilowatt-hour of solar production displaces expensive grid power. The state's SMART (Solar Massachusetts Renewable Target) program pays solar owners a fixed per-kWh incentive on top of net metering credits for 10 years, providing a reliable income stream that dramatically improves payback.

Massachusetts also maintains strong 1:1 net metering for systems under 10 kW, the state sales tax exempts solar equipment, and the property tax exemption means your home's increased value from solar will not raise your tax bill. The combination of these policies makes Massachusetts the best state in the Northeast for solar by a wide margin.

3. New York

Average electricity rate: $0.24/kWh | Peak sun hours: 3.8/day | 25-year savings: $35,000 - $50,000

New York's NY-Sun incentive program, administered by NYSERDA, provides upfront rebates of $0.20 to $0.40 per watt depending on your utility territory and system size. For a typical 8 kW residential system, that translates to $1,600 to $3,200 off the installation cost. Combined with the state's strong net metering laws and a 25% state tax credit (up to $5,000), New York homeowners can recoup their investment in 5 to 7 years.

Electricity rates in New York vary significantly by region. Downstate customers served by Con Edison pay among the highest rates in the country ($0.28 to $0.35/kWh), making solar especially attractive in the New York City metropolitan area and Long Island. Upstate rates are lower but still above the national average, and the NY-Sun rebates are typically higher for upstate territories to compensate.

4. Colorado

Average electricity rate: $0.15/kWh | Peak sun hours: 5.0/day | 25-year savings: $25,000 - $40,000

Colorado offers a compelling combination of strong sunshine and direct financial incentives. The state provides a $5,000 state tax credit for residential solar installations, which partially fills the gap left by the expired federal ITC. With an average of 5.0 peak sun hours per day and 300+ days of sunshine per year, Colorado systems produce reliably strong output.

Electricity rates are moderate compared to coastal states, averaging around $0.15/kWh, but Xcel Energy's rates have been climbing steadily and are projected to continue rising. Colorado also has net metering through 2026 for Xcel customers, though the program is under review. Homeowners who lock in solar now benefit from current net metering terms and the state tax credit, both of which could change in coming years.

5. Connecticut

Average electricity rate: $0.27/kWh | Peak sun hours: 3.8/day | 25-year savings: $35,000 - $48,000

Connecticut has some of the highest electricity rates in the continental United States, regularly exceeding $0.27/kWh and spiking above $0.30/kWh during winter months. This makes every kilowatt-hour of solar production exceptionally valuable. The state's Residential Solar Investment Program provides upfront incentives, and Connecticut also offers a sales tax exemption for solar equipment purchases and a property tax exemption for the added home value.

The state's net metering program provides full retail-rate credits for exported solar electricity, and Connecticut's Renewable Portfolio Standard continues to drive utility-scale and residential solar growth. While the state receives less sunshine than southern competitors, the high electricity rates more than compensate, making payback periods surprisingly short at 5 to 7 years for well-sized systems.

6. New Jersey

Average electricity rate: $0.18/kWh | Peak sun hours: 4.2/day | 25-year savings: $30,000 - $45,000

New Jersey punches well above its weight in solar thanks to one of the strongest SREC (Solar Renewable Energy Certificate) markets in the country. Under the Successor Solar Incentive (SuSI) program, residential solar owners earn Transition Renewable Energy Certificates (TRECs) worth roughly $90 to $100 each for every megawatt-hour their system produces. For a typical 8 kW system producing about 9.5 MWh per year, that is an additional $855 to $950 annually on top of electricity savings.

New Jersey also exempts solar equipment from state sales tax (saving 6.625% on the purchase) and provides a property tax exemption for the added value solar brings to your home. The state's net metering program credits exported energy at full retail rate, and electricity rates, while moderate by Northeast standards, are still above the national average.

7. Arizona

Average electricity rate: $0.14/kWh | Peak sun hours: 5.5+/day | 25-year savings: $25,000 - $38,000

Arizona has the best solar resource in the country, with Phoenix averaging 5.5 to 6.5 peak sun hours per day. A solar panel system in Arizona will produce 20% to 40% more electricity than the same system in a northeastern state. However, Arizona's relatively low electricity rates ($0.14/kWh average) partially offset this advantage.

Arizona's residential solar tax credit of 25% of system cost (capped at $1,000) is modest compared to other states on this list. The bigger draw is the sheer volume of production: a 7 kW system in Phoenix can produce over 12,000 kWh annually, compared to roughly 8,500 kWh for the same system in Boston. Arizona also has a property tax exemption for solar equipment. APS and SRP, the state's two largest utilities, have moved away from traditional net metering toward export rate structures that pay $0.03 to $0.05/kWh for excess energy, so pairing solar with battery storage is increasingly important here.

8. Texas

Average electricity rate: $0.14/kWh | Peak sun hours: 5.0/day | 25-year savings: $22,000 - $35,000

Texas does not offer a state income tax, which means there is no state-level solar tax credit. It also has a deregulated electricity market in most areas, so net metering policies vary by retail electricity provider rather than being mandated statewide. Despite these apparent disadvantages, Texas ranks in the top 10 for several reasons.

First, installation costs in Texas are among the lowest in the country, averaging $2.40 to $2.70 per watt compared to the national average of $2.85 to $3.10 per watt. Second, Texas receives excellent sunshine, with most of the state averaging 5.0+ peak sun hours per day. Third, some retail electricity providers in the deregulated ERCOT market offer competitive buyback rates for exported solar energy. Finally, Texas has no state sales tax on solar equipment and offers a property tax exemption. The combination of low costs and high production makes solar financially attractive even without direct state incentives.

9. Oregon

Average electricity rate: $0.13/kWh | Peak sun hours: 3.5/day | 25-year savings: $22,000 - $35,000

Oregon might seem like an unusual pick given its reputation for rain, but the state's incentive structure is among the best in the country. The Oregon Solar + Storage Rebate Program provides rebates of $0.34 to $0.60 per watt for solar installations, and the state offers an additional tax credit of up to $5,000 for residential solar systems. For a typical 7 kW system, these combined incentives can reduce the upfront cost by $5,000 to $9,000.

Oregon also has strong net metering that credits exported energy at full retail rate, and the state has no sales tax, which saves homeowners another 5-8% compared to states that tax solar equipment. While electricity rates are lower than coastal states and sun exposure is limited (particularly west of the Cascades), the generous incentives dramatically shorten payback periods. Eastern Oregon, which receives significantly more sun, is an especially strong solar market.

10. Florida

Average electricity rate: $0.15/kWh | Peak sun hours: 5.2/day | 25-year savings: $20,000 - $35,000

The Sunshine State lives up to its name with 5.2 average peak sun hours per day and consistent year-round solar production. Florida does not have a state income tax, so there is no state solar tax credit. However, the state exempts solar equipment from sales tax (saving 6%) and provides a property tax exemption for the added home value.

Florida's net metering program, which credits exported energy at full retail rate, is a critical component of solar economics here. FPL (Florida Power & Light), Duke Energy, and Tampa Electric all participate. Electricity rates are moderate at around $0.15/kWh, but they have been rising 3-5% annually, which improves the long-term value of solar. Florida's combination of excellent sun, no sales tax on solar, property tax exemption, and net metering makes it a solid choice despite the lack of direct state rebates.

The Federal ITC Is Gone: What That Means for You

From 2006 through 2025, the federal solar Investment Tax Credit was the backbone of residential solar economics. At its peak, it covered 30% of total installation costs -- a credit worth $6,000 to $9,000 on a typical residential system. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 had extended the 30% rate through 2032, but the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law in July 2025, accelerated the expiration of all IRA clean energy credits.

As of January 1, 2026, the residential solar ITC no longer exists at the federal level. This is the single biggest policy change affecting rooftop solar in a decade, and it shifts the financial calculus significantly:

  • Upfront costs are higher. A $25,000 system now costs the full $25,000 out of pocket (minus any state incentives), compared to $17,500 after the 30% federal credit in 2025.
  • State incentives matter more than ever. States with strong rebates, tax credits, and SREC programs now provide the primary mechanism for reducing installation costs.
  • Payback periods are longer in low-incentive states. In states without meaningful incentives and with low electricity rates, payback periods have stretched from 7-9 years to 10-14 years.
  • Solar is still financially positive in most states. Even without the federal credit, solar panels produce 25+ years of free electricity. In any state with rates above $0.12/kWh, the lifetime savings still exceed the installation cost.

Section 48E Commercial Leasing: A Workaround That Still Exists

While the residential ITC is gone, the Section 48E Clean Electricity Investment Credit for commercial and tax-exempt entities survived the OBBBA cuts. This has an indirect benefit for homeowners: solar leasing companies and power purchase agreement (PPA) providers can still claim the commercial credit on systems they own and lease to homeowners.

This means solar leases and PPAs remain available in all 50 states, often with $0-down offers. Under a lease or PPA, a third-party company owns the solar panels on your roof, claims the commercial tax credit, and sells you the electricity at a rate lower than your utility's. You do not own the system and cannot claim any tax benefits, but you also avoid the upfront cost entirely. For homeowners who cannot afford to purchase a system outright, leasing through Section 48E remains a viable path to solar savings in every state.

When Solar Is NOT Worth It

Solar panels are not a slam-dunk investment everywhere. Several factors can make going solar a poor financial decision:

  • Very low electricity rates. States like Louisiana ($0.10/kWh), Idaho ($0.10/kWh), and Wyoming ($0.11/kWh) have cheap grid electricity. When your utility rate is low, each kWh of solar production saves you very little, extending payback periods beyond 12-15 years.
  • Low sun + low rates. The worst combination is low sunshine and cheap electricity. States like Washington ($0.11/kWh, 3.5 sun hours) and North Dakota ($0.11/kWh, 4.0 sun hours) make it difficult to justify the investment purely on financial terms.
  • No net metering. A handful of states have eliminated or severely weakened net metering. Without fair compensation for exported energy, systems must be carefully sized to avoid overproduction, which limits savings potential.
  • Roof issues. Regardless of your state, heavy shading, a north-facing roof, or a roof that needs replacement within 5-10 years can undermine solar economics. Address roof issues before installing panels.
  • Short ownership horizon. If you plan to sell your home within 3-4 years, you may not recoup enough savings to justify the installation cost, though solar does increase home resale value by an average of $15,000 to $25,000 nationally.

Key Takeaway

If your electricity rate is below $0.11/kWh and your state offers no meaningful solar incentives, a solar lease or PPA (which requires no upfront investment) may be a better option than purchasing a system outright.

How to Maximize Your Solar Savings in Any State

Regardless of where you live, these strategies will help you get the most from a solar investment in 2026:

  1. Claim every available incentive. Use the DSIRE database to find every federal, state, local, and utility incentive you qualify for. Stack them -- many programs can be combined.
  2. Get multiple quotes. Installation costs vary by 20-30% between contractors. Get at least three quotes and compare not just price, but equipment quality, warranties, and installer reviews.
  3. Right-size your system. In states with weak net metering, design your system to match your consumption rather than overproducing. In states with strong net metering, slightly oversizing can maximize credits.
  4. Consider battery storage. In states like California with time-of-use rates and reduced net metering (NEM 3.0), adding a battery lets you store daytime solar production and use it during expensive evening peak hours.
  5. Monitor rate escalation. Electricity rates in most states are rising 2-5% annually. Your solar savings grow every year as the rate you are avoiding increases, while your panels continue producing at no marginal cost.
  6. Explore solar loans. Many credit unions and solar lenders offer loans at 3-6% interest with 10-25 year terms. Monthly loan payments are often less than the electricity bill they replace, creating immediate positive cash flow.

The Bottom Line

The loss of the federal solar ITC is a real setback, but it has not killed the financial case for rooftop solar. In the 10 states listed above, solar panels remain one of the best investments a homeowner can make, delivering $20,000 to $65,000 in savings over 25 years depending on your electricity rate, sun exposure, and available incentives.

The key insight for 2026 is that state-level policy now drives the economics. If you live in a state with high electricity rates, strong net metering, and meaningful incentives, solar is an excellent investment even without federal support. If you live in a state with low rates and minimal incentives, a solar lease or PPA through Section 48E can still deliver savings with zero upfront cost.

Either way, electricity rates are only going up. Every year you wait, you are paying your utility for electricity your roof could be producing for free. The best time to go solar was when the federal credit still existed. The second best time is now.
