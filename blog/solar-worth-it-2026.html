Is Solar Worth It in 2026 Without the Federal Tax Credit? Here's the Math

The 30% residential solar ITC is gone, but panel prices have never been lower. We run the numbers on payback, savings, and whether going solar still makes financial sense this year.

By GreenSage February 9, 2026 9 min read

If you've been thinking about solar panels, you've probably heard the bad news: the federal 30% residential Investment Tax Credit (ITC) expired on December 31, 2025. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, eliminated the residential clean energy credit as part of a broader tax restructuring.

That's a real loss. On a $25,000 solar installation, the ITC used to hand you $7,500 back at tax time. Now it's zero.

So the question everyone is asking: is solar worth it in 2026 without that credit?

The short answer is yes, for most homeowners. The longer answer involves some math, and that's what this article is for.

Key Takeaway

Even without the federal ITC, solar panels still pay for themselves in 6-10 years and generate 15-20+ years of free electricity after that. The payback period is longer than it was, but the investment remains one of the strongest you can make on your home.

What Changed: OBBBA and the End of the Residential Solar ITC

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 extended the residential clean energy credit at 30% through 2032. That timeline was cut short. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, passed by Congress and signed on July 4, 2025, repealed the residential ITC (Section 25D) effective December 31, 2025.

Here's what that means in practical terms:

  • Systems installed and operational by Dec 31, 2025 can still claim the 30% credit on their 2025 tax return.
  • Systems installed on or after Jan 1, 2026 receive no federal residential solar tax credit.
  • The commercial solar ITC (Section 48E) was not repealed and continues through 2027. This matters more than you might think (we'll explain below).
  • State-level incentives were unaffected by OBBBA and remain available in 18+ states.

The loss of the ITC is significant, but it's not the whole picture. Solar economics have fundamentally changed since the credit was first introduced, and today's numbers tell a different story than you might expect.

Solar Panel Costs Have Dropped 70% Since 2010

Here's the context that most "solar is dead" headlines leave out: the cost of residential solar has plummeted.

In 2010, the average installed cost of residential solar was around $7.50 per watt. In 2026, you're looking at $2.50 to $3.00 per watt fully installed, depending on your market, roof complexity, and the equipment you choose. That's a roughly 70% drop in real costs.

For a typical 8 kW system (enough to offset most of an average household's electricity consumption):

Year Cost per Watt 8 kW System Cost After 30% ITC
2010 $7.50 $60,000 $42,000
2018 $3.50 $28,000 $19,600
2024 $2.85 $22,800 $15,960
2026 $2.75 $22,000 $22,000 (no ITC)

Yes, the 2026 out-of-pocket cost ($22,000) is higher than the 2024 after-ITC cost ($15,960). But it's dramatically lower than what early solar adopters paid in 2010 even with the credit. The industry has matured. Panel efficiency has improved. Installation has become standardized. Competition among installers is fierce.

The Payback Math: Before OBBBA vs. After OBBBA

Let's run a realistic scenario for a homeowner in a mid-range solar market. We'll use an 8 kW system, an average electricity rate of $0.16/kWh, and 1,200 kWh of monthly consumption.

The payback period went from roughly 4-7 years (with ITC and state incentives) to 6-10 years (without ITC, depending on state incentives). That's a meaningful difference, but it doesn't break the investment case. A 25-30 year asset that pays for itself in under a decade is still an excellent deal.

Key Takeaway

The payback period is 2-3 years longer without the ITC, but the total lifetime savings are still $38,000-$55,000 for a typical system. Solar remains one of the highest-ROI home improvements you can make.

Electricity Rates Are Rising -- Solar Locks in Your Price

One of the most underappreciated arguments for solar is the hedge it provides against rising electricity costs. U.S. residential electricity rates have been climbing 3-5% per year on average. In some states, the increases have been much steeper -- California rates rose over 40% between 2020 and 2025.

When you install solar, you're essentially prepaying for 25-30 years of electricity at today's rates. Every year that utility rates go up, your effective savings go up with them. That's why the "average annual savings" figure in our table above ($2,200) is higher than the Year 1 figure ($1,680) -- it accounts for compounding rate increases over the system's lifetime.

If electricity rates rise at 4% annually, a homeowner paying $0.16/kWh today will be paying $0.43/kWh in 25 years. Your solar panels will still be producing power at an effective cost of $0.00/kWh.

Net Metering: Sell Your Excess Back to the Grid

Most states still offer some form of net metering, which allows you to send excess solar electricity back to the grid in exchange for credits on your utility bill. During sunny summer days, your system may produce more electricity than you use. Net metering means that surplus isn't wasted -- it offsets your electricity costs during cloudy days, nights, and winter months.

Net metering policies vary by state and utility. Some offer full retail-rate credits (the best deal), while others offer wholesale or avoided-cost rates. Before installing, check your utility's net metering policy, as it significantly affects your savings calculation.

States with strong net metering policies include California (under NEM 3.0, which reduced but didn't eliminate credits), New Jersey, Maryland, Massachusetts, Colorado, and New York.

Section 48E: The Commercial ITC Is Still Alive (and It Benefits You)

Here's something most homeowners don't realize: the commercial clean energy ITC under Section 48E was not repealed by OBBBA and remains in effect through at least 2027. Why does this matter for residential customers?

If you go solar through a solar lease or Power Purchase Agreement (PPA), the solar company owns the system and claims the commercial ITC. They then pass those savings through to you in the form of lower monthly payments. This means:

  • Third-party-owned solar (leases and PPAs) can still benefit from a federal tax credit in 2026.
  • Monthly PPA rates may be lower than you'd expect, because the installer is still getting their 30% credit.
  • You don't need to own the system to benefit from incentivized pricing.

The trade-off is that you don't own the panels, so you miss out on some of the long-term savings and the home value increase. But for homeowners who can't afford the upfront cost or don't want to take on a solar loan, a lease or PPA can still offer Day 1 savings on electricity bills in 2026.

Solar Adds ~4% to Your Home's Resale Value

According to a widely cited Zillow study, homes with solar panels sell for approximately 4.1% more than comparable homes without them. On a $400,000 home, that's roughly $16,400 in additional resale value.

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) has confirmed similar findings, estimating that home buyers are willing to pay a premium of approximately $4 per watt of installed solar capacity. For an 8 kW system, that's a $32,000 premium -- more than the system costs to install.

This means that even in a worst-case scenario where you sell your home before the payback period is complete, you're likely to recoup your investment (and then some) through the higher sale price. Solar isn't just an energy investment -- it's a home improvement that pays for itself at resale.

Key Takeaway

Solar panels increase your home's resale value by roughly 4%, according to Zillow. On a $400,000 home, that's $16,400 in added equity -- a significant offset to the installation cost even without the federal tax credit.

What About the New Auto Loan Interest Deduction?

You may have heard about another new incentive from OBBBA: the Auto Loan Interest Deduction, which allows taxpayers to deduct up to $10,000 per year in auto loan interest for vehicles assembled in the United States through 2028.

This deduction doesn't directly help solar buyers, but it's worth mentioning for two reasons:

  1. It shows Congress still values incentivizing green-adjacent purchases, even after repealing the clean energy credits. The auto loan deduction specifically targets U.S.-assembled vehicles, many of which are EVs and hybrids.
  2. If you're also shopping for an EV, the auto loan interest deduction may offset some of the sting of the expired EV tax credit. Pairing a solar installation with an EV (charged at home from your panels) remains one of the most powerful money-saving combinations available.

Solar plus EV is still the ultimate energy independence play. Your panels produce the electricity, your car runs on it, and your utility bill approaches zero.

When Solar Might NOT Be Worth It in 2026

We want to be honest: solar isn't the right move for everyone. Here are situations where the math may not work in your favor:

  • Very low electricity rates: If you pay less than $0.10/kWh (common in parts of the Southeast and Pacific Northwest), the payback period stretches beyond 12-15 years.
  • Heavy shading or poor roof orientation: If your roof faces north or is shaded by trees or buildings for much of the day, your production will be significantly reduced.
  • Planning to move within 3-5 years: While solar adds resale value, you may not recoup 100% of the cost if you sell very early, especially without the ITC.
  • Roof needs replacement soon: If your roof has less than 10 years of life left, factor in the cost of re-roofing (and removing/reinstalling panels) before committing.
  • No state incentives and no net metering: In states with minimal incentives and poor net metering policies, the payback period can be uncomfortably long.

For everyone else -- especially homeowners in states with good solar resources, rising electricity rates, and state-level incentives -- 2026 is still an excellent time to go solar.

The Bottom Line: Solar Is Still One of the Best Home Investments

Losing the 30% federal ITC hurts. There's no sugarcoating that. It adds roughly $6,000-$8,000 to the effective cost of a typical residential system and extends the payback period by 2-3 years.

But here's the bigger picture:

  • Panel costs have dropped 70% since 2010, making solar more affordable than ever in absolute terms.
  • Electricity rates are rising 3-5% per year, and solar locks in your rate for decades.
  • Systems last 25-30 years, meaning you'll enjoy 15-20+ years of free electricity after the payback period.
  • State incentives in 18+ states can cut your payback period by 2-4 years.
  • Solar leases and PPAs still benefit from the commercial ITC through 2027.
  • Home resale value increases ~4%, effectively insuring your investment.
  • Net metering lets you sell excess production back to the grid.

A 6-10 year payback on an asset that lasts 25-30 years still translates to a strong return on investment. In purely financial terms, it's comparable to a bond yielding 8-12% annually -- except this "bond" also protects you from rising energy costs and increases the value of your home.

Is solar worth it in 2026? For most homeowners, absolutely yes. The payback is longer, but the destination is the same: decades of free, clean electricity and tens of thousands of dollars in lifetime savings.
